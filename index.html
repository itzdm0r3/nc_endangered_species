Save The Endangered Species Of North Carolina


Introduction

The State of North Carolina is home to numerous endangered species. As of July 2016, 35 animals have been included on the list. Animals such as the Carolina Nothern Flying squirrel who make their home in the state have found themselves on the brink of extinction.

According to a report by the U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service, the habitats important to species like the Flying squirrel are under threat from environmental factors such as: pests, pollution, development, and climate change. If you would like to read a copy of the report click here.

Due to the continued raise in population throughout the Southern Appalachians, the displacement of these species is occuring at such an alarming rate that futher studies are taking place to see if recovery is even possible.

The purpose of this webpage is to spread awareness of the growing issue of species becoming endangered in North Carolina (and its surrounding areas) due to the above mentioned environmental factors.

Carolina Nothern Flying Squirrel


More information about the Carolina northern flying squirrel and the following animals
below can be found via the U.S. Fish & Wildlife Service website.

The nothern flying squirrel is a small nocturnal gliding mammal approximately a foot in length, and 3 to 5 ounces in weight.
It has long, broad, flattened tail that accounts for near 80% of its head and body length; prominent eyes, and dense, silky fur. According to the Fish & Wildlife Service, "the broad tail and folds of skin between the wrist and ankle form the aerodynamic surface used for gliding."

Colors of the adults range from gray with shades of brown and tan, with a reddish shade on the back; to grayish-white or buffy white. Juveniles are described having dark, slate gray backs and off-white undersides. The nothern flying squirrel is distinguishable from its southern cousin by it's larger size, and it's gray base of ventral hairs--in contrast to the white base on the southern variety--and it's longer upper tooth row amongst other differences.

Northern flying squirrels tend to make their homes in the North Carolina and Tennessee areas. As of the the time of this writing, there are no conservation plans or petitions available on this species. This makes them vulnerable to extinction as logging and population growth continue to displace them. However, in the areas the squirrels do habitate, they tend to be perserved for wildlife conservation.



Virgina Big-Eared Bat


You can find more information about the Virginia big-eared bat at NCWildlife.org

The Virginia big-eared bat is a rare inhabitant of the Northwestern mountains of North Carolina. Most populations of these species reside in the Western parts of the United States, with species living in the East being rare. An article on the species by NCWildlife.org states that biologist know of only one colony living in North Carolina. You can find the elusive creatures in the Grandfather Mountain and Beech Mountain areas of NC--all small cave in the Blue Ridge Parkway provides them with water. It is of note that conservationists have built a gate around the cave entrance in an effort out to keep visitors away.
